## Abstract
This document reexamines how effective carbon credits and carbon offsets have actually been as climate measures, using emissions trends, carbon pricing revenue, offset quality, and the more direct question of whether they have measurably cooled the planet.

The central argument is not that every carbon-credit mechanism is meaningless, but that current carbon-pricing and offset systems, even when they mobilize large sums of money, have not yet demonstrated a sufficient capacity to lower planetary heat in a clear and measurable way. For that reason, a new framework is needed: one that evaluates and rewards measurable cooling outcomes.

## Carbon-credit markets expanded, but global emissions remained high
The expansion of carbon-pricing systems is real. Carbon pricing now covers a substantial share of global emissions and has generated more than USD 100 billion annually in public revenue.

However, the growth of market coverage and revenue does not automatically mean that total global emissions have fallen fast enough, nor does it prove that the planet has actually cooled. Global CO2 emissions have remained at historically high levels, which makes it difficult to argue that market expansion by itself has delivered sufficient climate results.

### Key points
- Market growth and emissions reduction are not the same thing.
- Even when some advanced economies flatten or reduce emissions, global totals can remain high.
- Buying credits may improve accounting positions, but physical heat accumulation is a separate matter.

> A more defensible academic formulation is this: **rather than claiming that carbon credits are entirely useless, it is more accurate to say that their real-world effect on global emissions reduction and planetary cooling has remained limited relative to the scale of the problem.**

## Carbon offsets still face major issues of additionality, permanence, and over-crediting
A central question in the offset market is whether a credit truly represents an additional emissions reduction or removal that would not have happened otherwise.

The major methodological concerns are well known: additionality, leakage, permanence, time delay, and optimistic quantification. In practice, this means that not all offsets can be treated as equal in environmental quality, and not every credited unit should be assumed to represent a durable climate benefit.

### Technical issues
- **Additionality**: Would the project have happened even without credit revenue?
- **Permanence**: Can stored carbon be released again through fire, land degradation, drought, or future disturbance?
- **Leakage**: Does a reduction in one area create emissions elsewhere?
- **Conservative quantification**: Are claimed reductions or removals overstated?

Therefore, offsets may support claims of compensation on paper, but they do not automatically guarantee equivalent, lasting, and physically meaningful climate benefits.

## Why it is difficult to say that carbon credits have “cooled the Earth”
The final metric of climate success is not accounting neutrality, but changes in real heat accumulation across the atmosphere, land, and oceans.

Conventional carbon-credit systems mainly evaluate CO2-equivalent quantities. They do not directly evaluate urban waste heat, hydrological recovery, soil moisture restoration, evapotranspiration, ocean heat stress, or WBGT improvement as primary targets. As a result, even a growing carbon market may fail to correspond to measurable cooling in real environments.

### Structural blind spots
- Carbon accounting tracks carbon quantities, not heat itself.
- Netting-out on paper is not the same as restoring cooling functions in the real world.
- If the goal is climate recovery, policy should evaluate cooling-function restoration, not only emissions balances.

## Cooling Credits as an alternative design
Cooling Credits are proposed not as a simple rejection of carbon accounting, but as a complementary or higher-order framework that rewards measurable cooling outcomes.

Possible metrics include air-temperature reduction, surface-temperature reduction, WBGT improvement, water-cycle recovery, soil-moisture recovery, evapotranspiration recovery, reduced waste heat, and support for marine or ecological cooling functions. In other words, the core question becomes not only **“How many tons were offset?”** but also **“How much measurable cooling function was restored?”**

### Implementation examples
- Urban cooling infrastructure, mist systems, reflective surfaces, and shade expansion.
- Food-loss and organic-waste conversion into soil restoration that improves moisture retention and microbial activity.
- Forest, watershed, wetland, and landscape regeneration that restores evapotranspiration and local rainfall support.
- Ocean-circulation, oxygenation, and ecological restoration technologies linked to cooling support.

> **Defensive formulation**  
> “Current carbon-credit systems may have some value in mobilizing funds, but when judged against the goal of reducing planetary heat, their practical effectiveness remains insufficient. A framework that directly evaluates measurable cooling outcomes should therefore be considered.”

## Conclusion
Current carbon-credit systems have expanded as instruments of policy, finance, and corporate reporting, but expansion alone does not make them sufficient as climate solutions. As long as global emissions remain high and offset quality remains contested, it is difficult to treat the current model as a complete answer to global warming.

From the perspective of actually cooling the Earth, the next step is to move beyond accounting-only compensation and toward systems that directly measure and reward the restoration of cooling functions across heat, water, soil, ecosystems, cities, and oceans. Cooling Credits are proposed as one candidate framework for that transition.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### 1. Are carbon credits meaningless?
Not entirely. They can play a role in policy finance and emissions accounting. However, it is difficult to argue that market expansion by itself has produced sufficient global emissions reductions or measurable planetary cooling.

### 2. Do carbon offsets provide no environmental benefit at all?
Some individual projects may produce genuine benefits. The problem is that offset quality varies significantly, and additionality, permanence, leakage, and over-crediting remain serious concerns.

### 3. Why emphasize “cooling the planet” as a framing?
Because the ultimate outcome of global warming is physical heat accumulation, not just accounting imbalance. That is why temperature, surface heat, WBGT, water cycles, soil moisture, and ecological cooling functions deserve direct attention.

### 4. Do Cooling Credits reject CO2 reduction?
No. The idea is not to reject CO2 reduction, but to complement it by evaluating the next question: whether real cooling effects were achieved. Cooling Credits are designed to make heat reduction and cooling-function recovery visible and rewardable.

### 5. What kinds of companies could participate in Cooling Credits?
Not only heavy industry or energy companies. Urban cooling, construction, agriculture, food systems, waste management, water infrastructure, forestry, tourism, insurance, and local governments could all participate. The key is not only who reduces emissions, but who restores measurable cooling functions.
